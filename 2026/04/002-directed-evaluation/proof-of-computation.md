## What the substrate actually is

**Proof of computation, not secret-message-passing.** The substrate
gives a deterministic, verifiable artifact (V) that records *"a
computation happened under these conditions"* — but it doesn't hide
content from someone who has the form.

This single distinction resolves the symmetric/asymmetric flip-flop
that was happening across the prior beats. The substrate's
primitives compose into either symmetric or asymmetric *protocols*
— but the substrate itself is neither. It's a verifiable-computation
layer that protocols build on.

## What's NOT here (honest narrowing)

- **Encryption** — there is no decrypt operation. V does not recover
  F. You can't use the substrate to send Bob a secret message that
  only Bob (with the right key) can read. That requires inversion;
  the substrate's directed-graph property forbids it.
- **Zero-knowledge** — verification always requires the verifier to
  HAVE F. There's no protocol here that proves "I know F" without
  eventually showing F. ZK proofs would require additional substrate
  work (SNARKs, STARKs, or similar).
- **Classical PKI** — no algebraic key-pair generation. The seed K
  is symmetric in the sense that anyone with it can encode/verify;
  there's no derivation of a "public K" from a "private K" with
  algebraic asymmetry.

## What IS here (real cryptographic shapes)

Three shapes the substrate supports cleanly:

### 1. Commitment-then-reveal

- Alice keeps (F, K) private at time T1
- Alice publishes V at time T1 (the commitment)
- Alice reveals F (and possibly K) at time T2 > T1
- Anyone can now verify "Alice knew F by T1"

This is what blockchains do with hash commitments. The reveal LEAKS
F (and possibly K), but that's intentional — the protocol depends
on revealing.

Use cases:
- Time-stamped claims ("I had this prediction at time T")
- Sealed bids in auctions
- Patent-like priority claims for ideas/proofs
- Audit-time disclosure of pre-committed data

### 2. Audit / provenance via lattice

- A system records (V) entries in the lattice over time
- Each V was produced by some (F, K)
- Auditors arrive later, verify recorded V's against revealed (F, K)

This is commitment-then-reveal at scale. The lattice IS the
auditable log; its content-addressable structure means tampering is
detectable (recorded V doesn't match recomputed V on F + K).

Use cases:
- Tamper-evident computation logs
- Reproducible-computation guarantees
- Distributed accountability (anyone can verify; no central trust)

### 3. Symmetric authenticated artifacts within trusted-K groups

- Alice + Bob share K
- Alice computes F → V under K, sends V
- Bob has K and (eventually) F to verify

This is HMAC-shaped. Both parties trust the same secret; V serves
as authentication that the message was generated by someone in the
trusted-K group.

Use cases:
- Multi-party computation where participants share a universe
- Encrypted-at-rest within a closed system (vectors stored;
  recipients with K can re-derive)
- Authenticated channels within a federation

## What V buys (in the user's framing)

**Proof of completion.** V is a deterministic record that:
- A specific F was processed
- Under a specific K
- The result is reproducible by anyone with (F, K)

V is not a ciphertext. V is a fingerprint of computation.

The user's "proof of completion i guess?" is the cleanest framing.

## Why F transmission risk is real

If F is the secret (e.g., a private cryptographic preimage), then
transmitting F defeats the secret. The substrate provides:
- *Deferred reveal*: F can be kept private until verification time
- *Time-stamped commitment*: V is the proof that F was known earlier

But once F is revealed, anyone who got it knows it. There's no
mechanism to prove "I knew F" without revealing F. (That's
zero-knowledge, which we don't have.)

This is a **real limit** on the substrate's secrecy model. Use cases
that depend on permanent F-secrecy aren't well-served. Use cases
that depend on time-anchored commitments work great.

## Security model

The substrate's cryptographic value scales with **form complexity**,
not key length:

- **Trivial F** (e.g., `(+ 2 2)`) — easy to enumerate; an attacker
  with V and K can search candidate F's quickly.
- **Complex F** (large nested forms, custom tokens, lots of
  structural variation) — hard to enumerate; the search space at
  Kanerva-capacity is astronomical.
- **Universe size** — d=10000 holds ~100 reliable items, but the
  forms expressible up to that capacity are vastly more numerous
  than the items themselves (combinatorial in form structure).

So: keep F complex enough that brute-force F-search against a known
V is infeasible. The substrate doesn't enforce this; it's a
protocol responsibility.
